# Existing Translations - Reference Guide

## Purpose

This document catalogs major existing translations of 红楼梦 for reference. While our translation should be original work, understanding existing approaches helps make informed decisions.

**Note**: Do NOT copy from existing translations. Use them only for comparison and to understand translation challenges.

---

## English Translations

### The Story of the Stone (Hawkes/Minford)

**Translators**: David Hawkes (Vols 1-3), John Minford (Vols 4-5)
**Publisher**: Penguin Classics
**Dates**: 1973-1986
**Volumes**: 5

**Approach**:
- Literary, fluent English
- Attempts to recreate literary effects
- Extensive footnotes
- Translates many names meaningfully

**Characteristics**:
- "Bao-yu" (hyphenated names)
- Often translates puns creatively
- Poetry attempts English verse forms
- Considered the gold standard in English

**Example Style**:
> "A boy with a volume of Cervantes exits the building..."
(Not actual quote - style illustration only)

### A Dream of Red Mansions (Yang Xianyi & Gladys Yang)

**Translators**: Yang Xianyi and Gladys Yang
**Publisher**: Foreign Languages Press, Beijing
**Dates**: 1978-1980
**Volumes**: 3

**Approach**:
- More literal translation
- Academic/scholarly tone
- Preserves Chinese structure more
- Pinyin romanization

**Characteristics**:
- "Jia Baoyu" (standard pinyin)
- More direct translation of names
- Less creative liberty with puns
- Valuable for understanding original meaning

## Russian Translation

**Translator**: В. А. Панасюк (V. A. Panasyuk)
**Publisher**: Various Soviet/Russian publishers
**Date**: 1958 (first complete edition)

**Approach**:
- Literary Russian prose
- Soviet-era translation conventions
- Complete 120 chapters

**Characteristics**:
- Russian transliteration of names
- Adapts poetry to Russian verse traditions
- Cultural footnotes for Soviet readers

## Key Translation Challenges Across Languages

### Character Names

| Original | Hawkes | Yang | Our Approach |
|----------|--------|------|--------------|
| 贾宝玉 | Bao-yu | Jia Baoyu | Jia Baoyu + note |
| 林黛玉 | Dai-yu | Lin Daiyu | Lin Daiyu + note |
| 薛宝钗 | Bao-chai | Xue Baochai | Xue Baochai + note |

**Our Decision**: Use standard Pinyin (Yang style) with explanatory notes for name meanings.

### Name Puns

| Original | Meaning | Hawkes | Our Approach |
|----------|---------|--------|--------------|
| 甄士隐 | True events hidden | Zhen Shiyin (explained) | Zhen Shiyin + note |
| 贾雨村 | False words exist | Jia Yucun | Jia Yucun + note |

**Our Decision**: Transliterate, then explain the pun in translator notes.

### Poetry

| Approach | Hawkes | Yang | Our Approach |
|----------|--------|------|--------------|
| Rhyme | Attempts rhyme | Rarely rhymes | Attempt when natural |
| Meter | English verse | Free verse | Preserve rhythm |
| Allusion | Creative adaptation | Explained in notes | Notes with meaning |

**Our Decision**: Prioritize meaning, attempt form when possible, explain in notes.

### Titles

| Original | Hawkes | Yang | Our Approach |
|----------|--------|------|--------------|
| 红楼梦 | Story of the Stone | Dream of Red Mansions | Dream of the Red Chamber |
| 石头记 | Story of the Stone | Story of the Stone | Story of the Stone |

**Our Decision**: "Dream of the Red Chamber" for main title, as it's widely recognized.

## What to Learn from Existing Translations

### From Hawkes
- Literary creativity is possible and valued
- Footnotes help readers enormously
- Poetry CAN work in English

### From Yang
- Accuracy matters for scholarly use
- Consistent romanization aids research
- Direct approach has its virtues

### From Russian
- Adapting to target literary traditions works
- Full translation IS possible
- Reader annotations help bridge culture gaps

### From Japanese
- Shared heritage can be leveraged
- Don't over-translate what readers can understand
- Classical flavor can be preserved
